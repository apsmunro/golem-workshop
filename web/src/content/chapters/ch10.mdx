Ten pebbles thrown at five buckets can land all in the middle exactly one
way. They can land 2-2-2-2-2 in 113,400 ways. Nothing about the throwing
favors flatness; there are simply astronomically more ways for the pebbles
to spread than to stack. That count of ways, scaled and logged, is
entropy, and it turns a vague instinct — "don't assume more than you
know" — into arithmetic the workshop can run.

<Wide><EntropyPebbles /></Wide>

Lock the lever and the game sharpens. Among all shapes with a given mean,
one maximizes entropy, and it is never the shape with drama in it. This is
the maximum entropy principle: choose the distribution that can happen the
most ways subject to what you actually know. It is a device for
*confessing* constraints rather than smuggling in extras.

If that sounds abstract, here is the practical question it answers. Every
model you build must declare a distribution for its outcome — chapter 4
declared heights Gaussian, chapter 2 declared tosses binomial. On what
grounds? Picking a distribution *is* an assumption, and a strong one. The
maximum entropy principle gives the grounds: state the constraints you
genuinely believe (the outcome is a count with a ceiling; it has some
average rate; it is continuous with a known spread), then take the flattest
distribution that satisfies them. Anything else quietly asserts a
constraint you never stated and cannot defend.

## The usual suspects, re-arrested

Chapter 4 excused the Gaussian as a law of ignorance. Now the excuse
becomes a theorem: fix a mean and a variance and the maximum-entropy
distribution over the real line is exactly the normal. Every other shape
with that mean and variance claims something additional — skew, spikes,
thin tails — that your constraints never asserted. The binomial earns the
same defense: for binary trials with a fixed expected number of successes,
it is the flattest thing there is. The globe-tossing golem of chapter 2
was a maximum-entropy golem all along; nobody had said so yet.

The recipe generalizes. Constraints on counts with no upper bound and a
fixed mean rate give the Poisson. A fixed mean of a positive continuous
quantity gives the exponential. Each of the so-called exponential family
is the honest distribution for some simple statement of knowledge, which
is why they keep appearing: not convention, but confession.

## Bending the line into the world

A Gaussian mean can wander anywhere, so chapter 4 could set μ equal to a
line and walk away. Other outcomes are fussier. A probability cannot leave
[0, 1]; a rate cannot go negative. But the linear predictor α + βx roams
the whole real line, and sooner or later it will propose a probability of
1.4 or a rate of −3. Generalized linear models keep the linear machinery
and insert a hinge — the link function — between the line and the
constrained parameter:

$$
y_i \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p_i), \qquad \text{logit}(p_i) = \alpha + \beta x_i
$$

<Gloss>
The line lives on the log-odds scale, where it may roam all reals; the
inverse link folds it back into probability. Nothing about y is
transformed — only the parameter.
</Gloss>

The logit is the workhorse, so meet it properly. Take a probability p,
form the odds p/(1−p) — the gambler's phrasing, "three to one" — and take
the log. Now 0.5 maps to 0, certainty maps to ±infinity, and the whole
awkward [0, 1] interval has been unrolled onto the real line where lines
are allowed to live. The model is linear *there*, and the inverse mapping
squashes the line back into an S-shaped curve on the probability scale.

<Wide><LinkMorpher /></Wide>

Ride the morph slider a few times. Equal steps on the link scale compress
into nothing near the floor and ceiling — a one-unit push moves a
probability of 0.5 a lot, a probability of 0.97 barely at all, which is as
it should be: near-certain things are hard to make more certain. That
bunching *is* the model's built-in interaction. Even with no product term
anywhere, the effect of one predictor on the outcome scale depends on
where every other predictor has already pushed η. The probe makes it
numeric: β holds still while the outcome-scale slope goes wherever p(1−p)
sends it. Remember this in chapter 11, when coefficients on the logit
scale refuse to be percentage points.

The log link plays the same trick for rates: linear on log λ, exponential
on λ, so predictors act multiplicatively. Each unit of x scales the rate
by a constant factor rather than adding a constant amount. And it sets the
trap this chapter's header is drawn from: a prior that is flat on the
logit scale is not flat on the probability scale — it piles up against 0
and 1, certain the world is extreme. The general moral outlasts the
example. A link function is a change of scale, and priors, like effects,
must be judged on the scale where their consequences live, which is one
more job for prior predictive simulation.

chimpanzees. Normal(0, 1.5) on the log-odds scale: push it through the
inverse logit and sketch what it believes about the probability itself.
