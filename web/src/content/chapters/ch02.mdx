Hold a globe at arm's length and toss it. Catch it. Look at the spot under
your right index finger: water or land? Toss again. After nine tosses you
have a record — say W L W W W L W L W — and one question: how much of this
globe is covered in water?

That little ritual is the whole of Bayesian inference in miniature, and
this bench builds it twice. Once with your hands, counting paths through a
garden. Once with twenty lines of R.

## Small worlds and large worlds

The globe in your hands is the large world: wet, dented, indifferent to
your model. The golem lives somewhere else — a small world where exactly
one number, the proportion of water $p$, governs everything, every toss is
independent, and nothing exists that the model didn't name. Inside that
small world the golem's logic is flawless. Whether the small world has
anything to do with the large one is your problem, never the golem's, and
no amount of arithmetic will make that judgment for you.

Keep both worlds in view at once. The chapters ahead are full of golems
that were perfect in their small worlds and wrong about ours.

## The garden of forking data

Strip the machinery away and Bayesian updating is counting. Consider every
way the world could be; for each one, count the paths that produce exactly
the data you saw; believe each world in proportion to its surviving paths.

Try it. Pick a conjecture below, draw data, and watch the incompatible
paths wither. When you press "update again", this round's counts become the
next round's starting weights — which is the entire theory of learning from
experience, in one button.

Notice what you never needed: no formula, no derivation, no "statistics".
Relative numbers of ways. That is what a probability is about to be.

## From counting to the globe

Counts get unwieldy when the conjectures form a continuum, so we rename.
The paths-per-conjecture, standardized, becomes a **likelihood**. The
starting weights become a **prior**. The surviving plausibility becomes the
**posterior**. For $W$ waters in $N$ tosses:

$$
\Pr(p \mid W, N) = \frac{\binom{N}{W} p^{W} (1-p)^{N-W} \times \Pr(p)}{\Pr(W)}
$$

<Gloss>
Read it as the garden: (ways this p produces the data) × (prior weight on
this p), normalized so all the plausibility sums to one. The denominator is
only the normalizing chore.
</Gloss>

The golem for this chapter has three parts and you should be able to point
at each: a likelihood (binomial — tosses are exchangeable coin flips with
stickiness $p$), a parameter to learn ($p$), and a prior (flat, for now —
every $p$ starts with equal path counts, an assumption we will outgrow
within a chapter).

## Making it go: grid approximation

The continuum version of "list every conjecture" is a grid. Score each grid
point by likelihood × prior, normalize, done — a posterior you computed by
brute honesty. Run it; the code is editable, so break it and see.

<WebRRunner

  code={`p_grid <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 20)
prior <- rep(1, 20)
likelihood <- dbinom(6, size = 9, prob = p_grid)
posterior <- likelihood * prior
posterior <- posterior / sum(posterior)
round(posterior, 3)`}

Twenty points is coarse on purpose: you can read the whole posterior as a
list. Push `length.out` to 1000 and the list becomes a curve; nothing else
changes. Grid approximation dies of the curse of dimensionality around two
or three parameters, which is why sturdier engines arrive in Chapter 9 —
same posterior, better plumbing.

## Predict before you look

Six waters in nine tosses, flat prior. Before running anything: where does
the posterior for $p$ pile up, and how wide is the pile? Draw it.

Whatever your overlap score, it goes in your calibration ledger, and the
ledger is the point. Being well calibrated about uncertainty is a skill,
it is trainable, and almost nobody arrives with it.
