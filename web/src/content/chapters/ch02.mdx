Hold a globe at arm's length and toss it. Catch it. Look at the spot under
your right index finger: water or land? Toss again. After nine tosses you
have a record — say W L W W W L W L W — and one question: how much of this
globe is covered in water?

That little ritual is the whole of Bayesian inference in miniature, and
this bench builds it twice. Once with your hands, counting paths through a
garden. Once with twenty lines of R.

Before the counting starts, notice the shape of the question. You are not
asking "is the globe mostly water, yes or no?" You are asking how plausible
every possible answer is — 10% water, 50%, 71%, all of them at once — given
what nine tosses told you. Holding many candidate answers in mind
simultaneously, each with its own degree of plausibility, is the one mental
habit this whole course is built on. Everything else is bookkeeping.

## Small worlds and large worlds

The globe in your hands is the large world: wet, dented, indifferent to
your model. The golem lives somewhere else — a small world where exactly
one number, the proportion of water $p$, governs everything, every toss is
independent, and nothing exists that the model didn't name. Inside that
small world the golem's logic is flawless. Whether the small world has
anything to do with the large one is your problem, never the golem's, and
no amount of arithmetic will make that judgment for you.

The small world leaves things out on purpose. It doesn't know that your
thumb tends to land on the Pacific, that the globe is dented near Norway,
or that you got bored after toss six. If any of those matter, the golem's
flawless logic will produce a flawless answer to the wrong question.

Keep both worlds in view at once. The chapters ahead are full of golems
that were perfect in their small worlds and wrong about ours.

## The garden of forking data

Strip the machinery away and Bayesian updating is counting. Consider every
way the world could be; for each one, count the paths that produce exactly
the data you saw; believe each world in proportion to its surviving paths.

A path here is nothing exotic. Suppose a bag holds four marbles, some blue
and some white, and you draw three with replacement. If the bag were one
blue and three white, how many distinct sequences of draws could produce
blue-white-blue? Count them on your fingers: one way to draw the first
blue, three ways the white, one way the second blue — three paths. A bag of
three blues and one white offers nine. Nothing about the counting requires
a formula; it requires patience, which is what computers are for.

Try it. Pick a conjecture below, draw data, and watch the incompatible
paths wither. When you press "update again", this round's counts become the
next round's starting weights — which is the entire theory of learning from
experience, in one button.

## From counting to the globe

Counts get unwieldy when the conjectures form a continuum — the globe's
water proportion could be any number between 0 and 1, and you cannot list
infinitely many bags of marbles. So we rename the pieces and let algebra
carry the counting. The paths-per-conjecture, standardized, becomes a
**likelihood**: for each candidate value of $p$, how readily would that
world produce the exact data you saw? The starting weights become a
**prior**: how plausible was each candidate before this data arrived? And
the surviving plausibility becomes the **posterior**: what you believe
after the data have spoken. For $W$ waters in $N$ tosses:

$$
\Pr(p \mid W, N) = \frac{\binom{N}{W} p^{W} (1-p)^{N-W} \times \Pr(p)}{\Pr(W)}
$$

<Gloss>
Read it as the garden: (ways this p produces the data) × (prior weight on
this p), normalized so all the plausibility sums to one. The denominator is
only the normalizing chore.
</Gloss>

Those three names — prior, likelihood, posterior — will appear on every
bench for the rest of the course, so pin them down now with the globe in
hand. The prior is what you believed about $p$ before tossing. The
likelihood is the model's engine: it scores each candidate $p$ by how well
it explains W L W W W L W L W. The posterior is the updated belief, and it
is always a compromise between the other two, weighted by how much data you
have. Nine tosses move a prior a little; nine thousand tosses steamroll it.

The golem for this chapter has three parts and you should be able to point
at each: a likelihood (binomial — tosses are exchangeable coin flips with
stickiness $p$), a parameter to learn ($p$), and a prior (flat, for now —
every $p$ starts with equal path counts, an assumption we will outgrow
within a chapter).

## Making it go: grid approximation

The continuum version of "list every conjecture" is a grid. Instead of
considering every possible $p$ between 0 and 1 (there are infinitely
many), consider twenty of them, evenly spaced, and pretend they are the
only options. Score each grid point by likelihood × prior, normalize so
the scores sum to one, done — a posterior you computed by brute honesty.
Run it; the code is editable, so break it and see.

<WebRRunner

prior <- rep(1, 20)
likelihood <- dbinom(6, size = 9, prob = p_grid)
posterior <- likelihood * prior
posterior <- posterior / sum(posterior)
round(posterior, 3)`}

/>

Twenty points is coarse on purpose: you can read the whole posterior as a
list, and reading it teaches you what a posterior *is* — a plausibility
score next to each candidate answer, nothing more mysterious than that.
Push `length.out` to 1000 and the list becomes a curve; nothing else
changes. Grid approximation dies of the curse of dimensionality around two
or three parameters: a fine grid over ten parameters needs more points
than there are atoms in you, which is why sturdier engines arrive in
Chapter 9. Same posterior, better plumbing.

## Predict before you look

Six waters in nine tosses, flat prior. Before running anything: where does
the posterior for $p$ pile up, and how wide is the pile? Draw it. Resist
the urge to peek first; a sketch you make after seeing the answer trains
nothing.

Whatever your overlap score, it goes in your calibration ledger, and the
ledger is the point. Being well calibrated about uncertainty is a skill,
it is trainable, and almost nobody arrives with it. Most people draw their
first posterior far too narrow — sure of an answer nine tosses cannot
possibly justify. If that was you, you are in the majority, and the cure
is exactly this exercise repeated across seventeen chapters.
