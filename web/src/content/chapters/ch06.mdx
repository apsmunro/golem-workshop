Here is a fact that should worry you: among funded scientists, the ones who
are worse researchers tend to be better self-promoters, and vice versa — even
if the two traits are unrelated in the population. Nobody engineered this
correlation. Selection did. Grant committees admit people who are strong on
*either* axis, and once you look only at the admitted, being weak on one
implies being strong on the other. Condition on a collider and you conjure a
relationship from thin air. This chapter is a catalogue of ways good golems
go wrong, and every one is a version of that trick.

## The collider of selection

The scientists story is a collider: talent → funded ← promotion. Funding is
a common effect of both. Selecting on it — studying only funded people —
opens the path and correlates the causes. The same shape explains why
handsome actors seem less talented (both get you cast), why nutritious
restaurants seem to have worse ambiance, and why controlling for a
"downstream" variable so often backfires. Open the collider preset in the
sandbox from the last chapter and watch the association appear the instant
you condition.

## Post-treatment bias

Suppose you test a fungicide on plants and, being thorough, you control for
the fungus that remains at harvest. You have just deleted your own treatment
effect. The treatment works *through* reducing fungus; condition on fungus
and you block the very pathway you meant to measure.

$$
H_1 \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma), \qquad \mu = H_0 \cdot p, \qquad p = f(\text{treatment}, \text{fungus})
$$

<Gloss>
Growth depends on fungus, and treatment acts by changing fungus. Put fungus
in the model and treatment looks useless — not because it is, but because you
adjusted away its only route.
</Gloss>

The rule is blunt and worth memorizing: never condition on a variable that
sits between your cause and your effect. "Control for everything measured" is
not caution, it is a way to be precisely, defensibly wrong.

## The haunted DAG

The subtlest trap needs no mistake on your part, only an unmeasured cause.
Grandparents, parents, children: education flows down the generations, and an
unseen neighborhood effect touches both parents and children. Condition on
parents — which any sane model of a child would — and you open a path through
the invisible neighborhood, biasing the grandparent effect you were after.
The confounder you cannot see reaches through the collider you had to
condition on.

## The four elemental confounds

Everything in this chapter reduces to four shapes between two variables and a
third: the fork (common cause — condition to close), the pipe (mediator —
do not condition or you block the effect), the collider (common effect — do
not condition or you open a fake path), and the descendant (a proxy that
partially conditions on whatever it descends from). Learn to see these four
in any DAG and you can read off what to control for without fitting anything.
That skill — not the regression — is the Haunted DAG Charm.

## Forge it

The 6H problems hand you DAGs and ask for adjustment sets, then confront your
answer with data. Use the sandbox to check yourself: list the paths, classify
each node, close the back doors and only the back doors. The charm protects
the golems you build in every later chapter — measurement error, missing
data, and multilevel models are all, underneath, careful conditioning.
