Here is a fact that should worry you: among funded scientists, the ones who
are worse researchers tend to be better self-promoters, and vice versa — even
if the two traits are unrelated in the population. Nobody engineered this
correlation. Selection did. Grant committees admit people who are strong on
*either* axis, and once you look only at the admitted, being weak on one
implies being strong on the other. Condition on a collider and you conjure a
relationship from thin air. This chapter is a catalogue of ways good golems
go wrong, and every one is a version of that trick.

Walk the logic through by hand once, because it feels like a card trick
until you do. Take a hundred scientists with talent and self-promotion
dealt independently — knowing one tells you nothing about the other. Fund
the twenty with the highest combined score. Who makes the cut? The
brilliant promoters, the brilliant researchers, and nobody weak at both.
Inside the funded group, a researcher with modest talent *must* have been a
strong promoter to get in, and vice versa. The negative correlation is
real, in that subgroup, and it was created entirely by how the subgroup was
chosen. No causal arrow connects the two traits; the selection drew one in.

## The collider of selection

The scientists story is a collider: talent → funded ← promotion. Funding is
a common effect of both. Selecting on it — studying only funded people —
opens the path and correlates the causes. The same shape explains why
handsome actors seem less talented (both get you cast), why nutritious
restaurants seem to have worse ambiance (both keep a restaurant open), and
why controlling for a "downstream" variable so often backfires. Note the
unsettling part: you do not need to *put* the collider in a regression to
fall for this. Studying a selected group at all (the funded, the hired,
the surviving) is conditioning, done by the world before your data ever
arrived. Open the collider preset in the sandbox from the last chapter and
watch the association appear the instant you condition.

## Post-treatment bias

Suppose you test a fungicide on plants and, being thorough, you control for
the fungus that remains at harvest. You have just deleted your own treatment
effect. The treatment works *through* reducing fungus; condition on fungus
and you block the very pathway you meant to measure.

$$
H_1 \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma), \qquad \mu = H_0 \cdot p, \qquad p = f(\text{treatment}, \text{fungus})
$$

<Gloss>
Growth depends on fungus, and treatment acts by changing fungus. Put fungus
in the model and treatment looks useless — not because it is, but because you
adjusted away its only route.
</Gloss>

To see why, ask what the coefficient on treatment means in that model:
"the effect of treatment, comparing plants with the *same* amount of
fungus." But plants with the same fungus have, by definition, already
received whatever benefit the treatment delivers — the treatment's whole
job was to change the fungus. You are comparing treated and untreated
plants after the treatment's work has been held equal, and finding,
correctly, nothing left. The golem answered the question you asked. The
question was self-defeating.

Sketch that sentence before you trust it. The fungicide genuinely works —
that is what makes this trap cruel. In the model that conditions on
fungus, where does the treatment coefficient land, and how certain is the
golem about it?

A working treatment, a coefficient parked on zero, and a golem perfectly
confident in both — that is what controlling for the mechanism buys.

The rule is blunt and worth memorizing: never condition on a variable that
sits between your cause and your effect. "Control for everything measured" is
not caution, it is a way to be precisely, defensibly wrong.

## The haunted DAG

The subtlest trap needs no mistake on your part, only an unmeasured cause.
Grandparents, parents, children: education flows down the generations, and an
unseen neighborhood effect touches both parents and children. Condition on
parents — which any sane model of a child would — and you open a path through
the invisible neighborhood, biasing the grandparent effect you were after.
Why does conditioning open anything? Because parents are a collider on that
path: grandparents point into parents, and so does the neighborhood. Hold
parents fixed and the two causes start trading information, exactly like
talent and promotion inside the funded group. The confounder you cannot see
reaches through the collider you had to condition on.

worth more than one that produces a confident number.

## The four elemental confounds

Everything in this chapter reduces to four shapes between two variables and
a third. The **fork** (Z causes both X and Y: condition on Z to close the
spurious path). The **pipe** (X causes Z causes Y: do not condition on Z,
or you block the effect you wanted; this is the fungus). The **collider**
(X and Y both cause Z: do not condition on Z, or you open a fake path;
this is the grant committee). And the **descendant**, a child of one of
those nodes: conditioning on it acts like a weak version of conditioning on
its parent, so a descendant of a collider is still a trap, just a shallower
one. Learn to see these four in any DAG and you can read off what to
control for without fitting anything. That skill — not the regression — is
the Haunted DAG Charm.

## Forge it

The 6H problems hand you DAGs and ask for adjustment sets, then confront your
answer with data. Use the sandbox to check yourself: list the paths, classify
each node, close the back doors and only the back doors. The charm protects
the golems you build in every later chapter — measurement error, missing
data, and multilevel models are all, underneath, careful conditioning.
