Rough terrain makes a country poorer. Bad soils, high transport costs,
isolation from trade — the mechanism is not mysterious, and outside Africa
the data agree: more ruggedness, less GDP. Inside Africa the slope points
the other way. Rugged highlands were harder for slave raiders to reach, and
that grim protection shows up in national income centuries later. One
predictor, two opposite effects, and no single line can say both things.

A golem that must give ruggedness one slope for the whole world will split
the difference and be wrong everywhere. What it needs is permission for the
slope to depend on something else. That permission is an interaction.

Until now, every model has been additive: each predictor contributes its
effect regardless of what the other predictors are doing, and the
contributions stack like bricks. An interaction breaks that truce. It says
the effect of one variable *is a function of* another — ruggedness helps
or hurts depending on where you are, medicine works or doesn't depending
on the dose of something else. Most interesting scientific claims have
this shape, which is why additive models so often feel like they are
answering a smoothed-over version of your question.

## One dataset, two worlds

$$
\mu_i = \alpha_{\text{cid}[i]} + \beta_{\text{cid}[i]} \left( r_i - \bar{r} \right)
$$

Each continent group gets its own intercept and its own ruggedness slope.
The interaction is the difference between the two betas.
</Gloss>

## When both dials are continuous

The Africa split was the easy case: the moderating variable was a category
with two levels, so "the slope depends on continent" meant two slopes. The
tulips make the harder case, where the moderator is itself continuous.
Blooms respond to water and to shade, but water can do nothing for a plant
in the dark: light and water are joint requirements, not independent
contributions. So the effect of water must shrink as shade deepens — a
continuous interaction, one slope sliding smoothly along another
variable's whole range, with no clean split anywhere.

<Wide><TulipsSurface /></Wide>

Drag the shade slider. In full sun the water slope is steep; two steps of
shade later it has flattened toward nothing. The model behind this adds a
single term, β<sub>ws</sub>·water·shade, and that one product carries the
whole biology. If the product term is unintuitive, multiply it out: the
water slope is now β<sub>w</sub> + β<sub>ws</sub>·shade, a quantity that
changes as shade changes. That is all an interaction term ever is — a slope
that has its own slope.

## Interactions are symmetric

The golem holds one equation, so "shade changes the effect of water" and
"water changes the effect of shade" are the same claim — µ contains
β<sub>ws</sub>·water·shade either way you read it. Which telling is useful
depends on your question, not on the math. This symmetry is also a warning:
you cannot read an interaction off a table of coefficients. The effect of
water is now β<sub>w</sub> + β<sub>ws</sub>·shade, a quantity that does not
sit in any single row of a summary — β<sub>w</sub> alone is only the water
slope at exactly zero shade, one point on a whole line of slopes. Plot
conditional predictions, as both panels above do, or you are interpreting
numbers you cannot see.

Two habits make interactions tractable. Center your predictors, so the
"main effect" coefficients mean something at a real point of the data
rather than at an imaginary zero — uncentered, β<sub>w</sub> describes
plants at zero shade, a condition your greenhouse may not even contain.
And run prior predictive simulations: interaction terms compound, and
priors that look tame on each coefficient can imply tulip beds that bloom
at negative rates.

## Forge it

8E and 8M are reasoning problems — name the third variable, write the
model, say what the product term claims about the world. The 8H problems
take you back to the tulips (add the greenhouse bed), back to rugged (find
the one country PSIS keeps complaining about, then decide what to do about
it), and out to new data on languages and wine. Every one of them comes
down to the same move: let the slope have a slope, then plot what the golem
actually believes.
