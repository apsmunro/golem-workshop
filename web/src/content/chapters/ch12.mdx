The pure golems of chapter 11 assume the world is tidier than it is. Real
counts scatter wider than a Poisson allows; real zeros arrive from more
than one source; real survey answers come as ordered grades that are not
numbers at all. This chapter builds monsters — hybrid golems stitched
from simpler ones — and the surprise is how principled the stitching is.
A mixture is not a hack. It is a likelihood that admits the data had more
than one way of happening.

## When variance outruns the mean

A Poisson insists variance equals mean: a rate of 10 per day should
scatter about ±3 around 10, no negotiation. Data rarely agree, and the
usual reason is hiding in plain sight — the single rate λ was a fiction.
Rates differ across units in ways no predictor captures: beaches with
different tides, monasteries with different scribes, each running its own
λ while the model insists there is only one. Force one rate onto many and
the pooled data spread wider than any single Poisson can explain. The
condition is called over-dispersion, and it is less a pathology than a
message: there is variation here you have not modeled.

The repair is to say so in the likelihood. Let each unit draw its own rate
from a distribution: binomial probabilities from a beta, Poisson rates
from a gamma. The result — beta-binomial, gamma-Poisson — is a continuous
mixture: fatter tails, honest intervals, and far less panic over
outliers, because an extreme unit is now read as "a unit with an unusual
rate" rather than "an impossibility". UCBadmit refit as a beta-binomial
stops being scandalized by department A. The cost is interpretive:
parameters now describe a *population* of rates, one more link in the
chain between coefficient and consequence.

## Two kinds of nothing

The monks of the scriptorium finish manuscripts at some daily rate — on
the days they work. Some days they drink. A day with zero manuscripts is
therefore ambiguous evidence, and a model that treats every zero as "slow
day" will get the work rate wrong while failing to predict the pile of
zeros in the ledger.

$$
\Pr(0) = p + (1 - p)\,e^{-\lambda}
$$

<Gloss>
Nothing happens two ways: the drinking way, with probability p, and the
working-but-unlucky way. The mixture credits each zero to both, in
proportion.
</Gloss>

Notice what the model is doing that you could not do by hand: no observer
can look at a particular zero and say which kind it was. The mixture never
needs to know. It splits each zero's evidential weight across the two
stories, in proportion to how plausible each story is given everything
else — and out come two clean estimates, the drinking probability and the
true work rate, neither of which appears in any column of the data.

brms carries a whole shelf of these mixed families — zero-inflated,
hurdle, beta-binomial via `beta_binomial()`, gamma-Poisson as
`negbinomial()` — each just a likelihood with an honest story about how
observations arise. HMC never sees the discrete "was it wine?" state;
the family marginalizes it out before sampling, which is why the golem
runs at all.

## Grades are not numbers

The Trolley survey asks for moral judgment on a 1-to-7 scale. It is
tempting to feed those responses to a Gaussian regression as if they were
measurements, and the temptation should be resisted: category 4 is not
twice category 2, and the psychological distance from 1 to 2 need not
equal the distance from 4 to 5. The only structure the data genuinely
carry is order. The ordered-categorical golem respects exactly that and
nothing more. Picture a hidden continuous axis of disapproval, and six
cutpoints carving it into seven bins; a response of "5" means only that
this person's judgment landed between cutpoint four and cutpoint five.
Cumulative log-odds is the machinery that makes the picture fittable.

<Wide><CutpointDragger /></Wide>

Drag until the bars agree, then reveal: the fitted cutpoints are nothing
but logits of cumulative proportions — the running totals of the observed
response frequencies, translated onto the log-odds scale. No magic was
performed. Predictors never touch the cutpoints — they shift φ, sliding
the whole story along the axis, leaking probability across every
threshold at once, which is exactly how a real change of attitude should
behave: every category boundary feels it simultaneously. Watch the
intended-contact cell: its histogram wants the density shoved hard left,
which is exactly what m12.5's most negative coefficient does.

    BI <- bI + bIA * A + bIC * C,
    c(bA, bI, bC, bIA, bIC) ~ dnorm(0, 0.5),
    cutpoints ~ dnorm(0, 1.5)
  ), data = dat, chains = 4, cores = 4)`}

  response ~ action + contact + intention +
    intention:action + intention:contact,
  data = d, family = cumulative("logit"),
  prior = c(
    prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = b),
    prior(normal(0, 1.5), class = Intercept)
  ),
  chains = 4, cores = 4, backend = "cmdstanr", seed = 1959
)`}

Ordered *predictors* get the mirror treatment, and the same refusal to
invent arithmetic. Education runs from primary school to advanced degree,
and coding it 1 through 8 would assert that every step is worth the same.
Instead the levels enter as cumulative increments δ — each step
contributes its own share of the total effect — with a Dirichlet prior
over how the shares split. The data decide whether the college step dwarfs
the middle-school step or the reverse. Order in, order out, arithmetic
nowhere.

## Forge it

The 12E problems make you say what over-dispersion is and which monster
eats it. 12M has you compute an ordered-categorical likelihood by hand
once — after the dragger, it is bookkeeping. The 12H set runs from
Hurricanes to bonobos: real over-dispersed, zero-inflated, ordered data,
each punishing the pure golem and rewarding the right monster. Finish
them and the Monster Mixer joins the bestiary, ladle first.
