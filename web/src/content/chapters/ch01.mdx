In the old story, Rabbi Judah Loew ben Bezalel shaped a man from Vltava clay
and wrote truth on its forehead — *emet* — and the thing stood up and worked.
It guarded the ghetto. It carried water. It followed instructions with total
fidelity and zero judgment, which is why the story ends with the rabbi
scraping one letter off its forehead, turning truth into death, and letting
the clay fall still. A servant that cannot tell a good order from a bad one
is dangerous exactly in proportion to its obedience.

You are here to build such servants. Every statistical model is a golem:
tireless, literal, incapable of noticing that the question you asked is a
silly one. It will estimate the effect you specified from the data you
handed it, and it will do so with the same untroubled confidence whether
your design is sound or an elaborate way of fooling yourself. The golem
never lies. It also never tells you the truth, exactly — it tells you what
follows from the clay you shaped.

If "statistical model" still feels abstract, make it concrete. A model is a
small machine with a slot on each end. Into one slot you feed data — heights,
counts, survey answers. Out of the other comes a summary the machine was
built to produce: an average difference, a slope, a probability. Everything
interesting lives in the middle, in the assumptions you built the machine
from. Feed the same data to two machines built from different assumptions
and you get two different answers, and the machines will never tell you
which set of assumptions was wiser. That part stays your job, permanently.

This first bench holds no tools, only a warning label written in someone
else's handwriting a century ago and never revised.

## The tests are not the science

Somewhere in your education you probably met a flowchart: which statistical
test goes with which kind of data. Two groups and a continuous outcome? The
t-test. Counts in categories? Chi-squared. More than two groups? ANOVA. That
chart is a bestiary of pre-built golems, each forged long ago for a narrow
chore, each still obeying. The chart teaches you to pick a servant. It does
not teach you to build one, so when your problem wanders off the chart (it
will, by roughly Chapter 5), you are left knocking on the doors of dead
engineers.

Each of those classical tests runs on the same fuel: a null hypothesis, a
deliberately boring stand-in claim that nothing is going on — no difference
between groups, no relationship between variables. The test asks how
surprising your data would be if the boring claim were true, and if the
answer is "quite surprising", you are invited to reject the boring claim and
feel that you have learned something.

The deeper trouble is that this ritual quietly claims to falsify hypotheses,
and it can't, except in cartoons. Any interesting scientific hypothesis is
consistent with several process models — several distinct stories about how
the world might generate data. Each of those stories maps onto several
statistical models, and the arrows run both directions and cross. So when
you reject a null, what exactly died? You have learned that one particular
golem, fed one particular dataset, produced a surprising number. The
hypothesis you actually care about may be sitting comfortably behind three
other process models the test never touched. Frequently, nothing died at
all, and the ceremony only felt like science.

None of this makes the classical tests wrong. A t-test is a perfectly good
golem for the chore it was built for. The mistake is treating the chart of
tests as if it were the reasoning, when it is only a cabinet of tools with
the reasoning removed.

## What we do instead

This workshop teaches golem engineering, one owner's manual at a time. Four
ideas run through everything:

- **Bayesian data analysis.** Strip away the notation and it is a counting
  exercise: consider every way the world could be, count the ways each
  version of the world could have produced the data you saw, and believe
  each version in proportion to its count. Explanations with more surviving
  ways earn more of your belief. Chapter 2 does this literally, with
  marbles, before any formula appears.
- **Multilevel models.** Golems with memory. Ordinary regression treats
  every group in your data — every pond, every hospital, every participant —
  as if it were the first one ever observed, learning nothing from the
  others. Multilevel golems let groups share information, which turns out to
  be the sane default rather than an advanced trick. The course's own author
  calls single-level regression the exception, and by Chapter 13 you will
  agree.
- **Model comparison by predictive accuracy.** When two golems disagree, you
  need a way to compare them that doesn't reward the one that flatters your
  data most. The honest question is: how wrong will each golem be about data
  it has not seen yet? Information theory turns that question into a number,
  minus the mysticism.
- **Graphical causal models.** A golem that predicts well can still answer a
  *why* question backwards. Barometers predict storms without causing them,
  and a model that confuses those two things will confidently recommend
  smashing barometers. Prediction and causation part ways in Chapter 5,
  loudly, and simple cause-and-effect diagrams become the tool for keeping
  them apart.

The engine under everything is **brms**, an R package that lets you describe
a model in one compact formula line and then hands the hard computation to
Stan, a battle-tested sampling engine. You will write models as formulas,
state your assumptions out loud, and let Stan do the heavy lifting. The
book's own code appears only as a dialect toggle for cross-reference — every
model in this course exists in both tongues, side by side.

## How a bench works

Every chapter in this workshop runs the same loop. You **predict** — sketch
an answer, pick a direction, commit — before any result is shown. This is
not a quiz to pass. Committing first is what makes the reveal informative:
if you never say what you expect, you can never be surprised, and surprise
is the only feedback that recalibrates your instincts. The app scores how
well your expectations track the mathematics over the whole course.

You **simulate**, because pushing numbers through a model teaches more than
reading about the model ever will. A model you have only read about is a
rumor; a model you have broken and repaired is a tool.

Then you **forge**: finish the work and the chapter's golem joins your
bestiary, permanently, with its formula stamped on its chest.

Your first golem waits at the next bench, and it is small: a fist of clay
that does nothing except toss a globe and count. Build it well. Every
monster you make afterward is this one with more letters on its forehead.
