In the old story, Rabbi Judah Loew ben Bezalel shaped a man from Vltava clay
and wrote truth on its forehead — *emet* — and the thing stood up and worked.
It guarded the ghetto. It carried water. It followed instructions with total
fidelity and zero judgment, which is why the story ends with the rabbi
scraping one letter off its forehead, turning truth into death, and letting
the clay fall still. A servant that cannot tell a good order from a bad one
is dangerous exactly in proportion to its obedience.

You are here to build such servants. Every statistical model is a golem:
tireless, literal, incapable of noticing that the question you asked is a
silly one. It will estimate the effect you specified from the data you
handed it, and it will do so with the same untroubled confidence whether
your design is sound or an elaborate way of fooling yourself. The golem
never lies. It also never tells you the truth, exactly — it tells you what
follows from the clay you shaped.

This first bench holds no tools, only a warning label written in someone
else's handwriting a century ago and never revised.

## The tests are not the science

Somewhere in your education you probably met a flowchart: which statistical
test goes with which kind of data. That chart is a bestiary of pre-built
golems — the t-test, the chi-squared, ANOVA — each forged long ago for a
narrow chore, each still obeying. The chart teaches you to pick a servant.
It does not teach you to build one, so when your problem wanders off the
chart (it will, by roughly Chapter 5), you are left knocking on the doors
of dead engineers.

The deeper trouble is that the chart quietly claims statistical tests can
falsify hypotheses. They can't, except in cartoons. Any interesting
hypothesis is consistent with several process models; each process model
maps to several statistical models; the arrows run both directions and
cross. Reject a null and you have learned that one particular golem, fed
one particular dataset, produced a surprising number. Which scientific
claim died? Frequently: none.

## What we do instead

This workshop teaches golem engineering, one owner's manual at a time:

- **Bayesian data analysis.** Count all the ways the data could have
  happened; prefer the explanations with more ways. Chapter 2 does this
  literally, with marbles.
- **Multilevel models.** Golems with memory, which resist the amnesia of
  treating every cluster as brand new. The course's own author calls
  single-level regression the exception, and by Chapter 13 you will agree.
- **Model comparison by predictive accuracy.** Information theory, minus
  the mysticism: how wrong will this golem be about data it has not seen?
- **Graphical causal models.** Because a golem that predicts well can still
  answer a *why* question backwards. Prediction and causation part ways in
  Chapter 5, loudly.

The engine under everything is **brms**: you will write models as formulas,
state priors out loud, and let Stan do the heavy lifting. Book code appears
only as a dialect toggle for cross-reference.

## How a bench works

Every chapter in this workshop runs the same loop. You **predict** — sketch
a posterior, pick a direction, commit — before any answer is shown, and the
app scores your calibration over the whole course. You **simulate**,
because pushing numbers through a model teaches more than reading about
the model ever will. Then you **forge**: finish the work and the chapter's
golem joins your bestiary, permanently, with its formula stamped on its
chest.

Your first golem waits at the next bench, and it is small: a fist of clay
that does nothing except toss a globe and count. Build it well. Every
monster you make afterward is this one with more letters on its forehead.
