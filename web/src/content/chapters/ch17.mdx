There is no golem to forge from a formula here. Chapter 17 has no models,
because its subject is the one thing no model contains: judgment. A golem
does exactly what its structure tells it to, with no sense of whether the
question was worth asking or the answer worth trusting. That sense stays with
you, and this chapter is where the course hands it back.

## The horoscope problem

A horoscope is unfalsifiable by design — vague enough to feel true whatever
happens. "You will face a challenge and grow from it" cannot be wrong,
which is exactly why it tells you nothing. A surprising amount of
statistical practice works the same way, and usually without anyone
intending it. A model flexible enough to absorb any result explains
nothing, though it fits everything. A workflow with enough quiet choices
(which cases to drop, which covariates to try, when to stop collecting)
will find a significant effect in pure noise, because somebody kept
turning dials until one lit up. A default prior dropped in without thought
is a wish dressed as knowledge. None of these announce themselves; each
feels like ordinary competent work from the inside.

The antidote is everything you built. Predict before you reveal, so the
data can surprise you. Simulate before you fit, so you know what your
model believes before it meets reality. Draw the causal graph, so you
adjust for confounds and not for mediators. Check the model against data
it has never seen, so flexibility cannot masquerade as fit. Each of those
habits was a chapter; together they are the difference between science and
astrology done with matrices.

The calibration number is not a grade. It is a mirror: it shows where your
sense of uncertainty tracked the truth and where it ran too narrow, which is
the most common way to be wrong — humans are reliably overconfident, drawing
intervals too tight and then acting surprised. A well-calibrated forecaster
who says "70%" is right about seventy percent of the time — no more, and no
less; the ones who claim 90% and are right half the time are not bolder,
just miscalibrated. That habit, more than any single model, is what the
course was training. It transfers to every decision you make under
uncertainty, most of which will never involve a posterior at all.

## What the golems cannot do

Each golem in your bestiary has a horizon set by its structure, not its
sample size. That is the point most easily forgotten, because more data
feels like it should cure everything. It cannot cure the wrong question. The Gaussian golem predicts height from weight and can never tell
you whether feeding a child changes its adult height; that is a causal
question its structure does not encode, and a billion rows would not add
the missing arrow. The compass of chapter 7 ranks models for prediction
and is silent on cause. The multilevel golems pool across clusters and
assume the clusters are exchangeable — interchangeable in the ways that
matter — which is sometimes exactly wrong. Knowing which golem to
distrust, and when to reach for another, is the wisdom the workshop cannot
forge for you. It is the part you supply.

## Forge it

The three bench drills close the loop. Read your own calibration and diagnose
its worst chapter; name a golem's structural limit and the golem you would
need instead; and find one place in your own work where a default still stands
in for a scientific model you have not written down. Finish them and the
Workshop Master Seal is stamped — not on a creature, but on you.
