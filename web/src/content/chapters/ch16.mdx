Every model so far has been a golem stitched from generic parts: link
functions, linear predictors, distributions chosen for their entropy. They
work because they assume little. This chapter does the opposite. It starts
from what you know about the thing itself — the geometry of a body, the
mechanics of a nut, the arithmetic of predators eating prey — and builds a
model shaped like the science. These golems assume a great deal, and when the
assumptions are right they see further than any curve fit.

The trade is worth naming plainly. A generic model asks the data to supply
the shape of the relationship, spending parameters to bend a flexible curve
into whatever the points suggest. A scientific model brings the shape and
asks the data only to fill in a constant or two. When your scientific story
is right, this is a spectacular bargain — you extrapolate where a curve fit
would flail, and your parameters mean something you can argue about with a
biologist. When the story is wrong, you fail loudly and specifically, which
is its own kind of gift.

## People are cylinders

Why should weight rise with height? Not for any statistical reason. A body is
roughly a cylinder of fixed proportions and fixed density, so its mass grows
with its volume, and its volume grows with the cube of its height. Double a
person's height while keeping their proportions and you get eight times the
volume, hence eight times the weight. That exponent — three — is not a
coefficient to estimate; it is geometry, fixed before any data arrive.

$$
W_i \sim \text{LogNormal}\!\left(\log\!\big(k\,\pi\,p^2\,H_i^{3}\big), \sigma\right)
$$

<Gloss>
Weight is proportional to height cubed times a constant that folds in
density and the width-to-height ratio p. Only the constant is fit; the
exponent 3 is brought, not learned.
</Gloss>

<Wide><GeometricPeople /></Wide>

Drag the exponent away from 3 and the fit falls apart at both ends at once —
the infants and the tallest adults. Return it to 3 and a single constant
carries the model across the whole growth range, no separate children's model
required. That is the payoff of a scientific model made visible: one number
does what a polynomial would need several wiggles to fake, and it keeps
working past the edges of the data because the cube is a fact about
geometry, not a pattern read off these particular people.

## Some models must be solved

Hares breed; lynx eat hares and breed; lynx starve. That story is not about
how many hares there are — it is about how fast the numbers *change*, and
how each population's rate of change depends on the other's size. Write it
that way and you get two coupled differential equations. They have no
closed-form solution: there is no tidy formula for "hares at year t". To
predict next year's hares you have to integrate the system forward, stepping
through time and letting each population push the other.

$$
\frac{dH}{dt} = H\,(b_H - m_H L), \qquad \frac{dL}{dt} = L\,(b_L H - m_L)
$$

<Gloss>
Hares grow at rate b_H and die as lynx L rise; lynx grow by eating hares H
and die at rate m_L. No formula gives H at time t — the golem integrates the
equations to find out.
</Gloss>

<Wide><LynxHareOde /></Wide>

The four rates do not set the populations directly; they set the shape of
the loop the populations trace as they chase each other — hares boom, lynx
follow, hares crash, lynx starve, hares recover. Plot lynx against hares and
that loop becomes a closed orbit, the phase portrait, which is the model's
real object. The Hudson's Bay pelt record cycles through it roughly every
ten years. Fitting this means solving the differential equations inside the
sampler at every step: the golem of this chapter does calculus to make a
prediction, and the reward is parameters that mean something ecological
(birth rates, death rates, predation efficiency) rather than coefficients
that mean nothing in particular.

## Forge it

The 16M problems free the height exponent to test whether the data endorse the
cube, check the mechanistic model's priors against physically possible people,
and perturb the lynx–hare rates. The 16H set fits real scientific models: the
Panda nut-opening data as a strength-versus-age growth curve, and the
lynx–hare equations against the pelt record end to end. Finish them and the
Mad Scientist Golem joins the bestiary, a cylinder for a body and an orbit for
a halo.
