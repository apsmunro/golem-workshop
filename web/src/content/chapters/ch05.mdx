States with more Waffle Houses have higher divorce rates. The correlation is
real, strong, and completely stupid: Waffle House is a Southern institution,
the South marries young, and marrying young predicts divorce. The waffles are
innocent bystanders standing near the crime. This chapter is about golems
that can tell a bystander from a cause — and about how easily they fail to.

## More is not better

The reflex when a variable looks spurious is to "control for" everything in
sight. Throw age, marriage rate, region, and waffles into one regression and
let the golem sort it out. Sometimes this works. Sometimes it manufactures
confounds, hides real effects, or flips a sign. A regression coefficient is
not "the effect of X"; it is "the effect of X, given exactly the other
things in this particular model", and that clause does enormous, invisible
work.

## Draw the causes first

Before any regression, draw what you think causes what. A directed acyclic
graph is a set of claims precise enough to be wrong: arrows for direct
causes, no cycles, and every path between two variables is either open
(carrying association) or blocked. The sandbox below lets you condition on
nodes and watch the doors. Start with waffles: which variables must you hold
fixed to ask honestly whether Waffle Houses cause divorce?

Two rules do most of the work. Conditioning on a **fork** (a common cause)
closes the spurious path — this is legitimate control, and it is what
dissolves the waffle correlation. Conditioning on a **collider** (a common
effect) *opens* a path that was closed, inventing association from nothing.
The difference between wisdom and self-sabotage is knowing which kind of node
you just clicked.

## Multiple regression, read honestly

Fit divorce on marriage rate alone and it looks important. Add median age at
marriage and the marriage-rate coefficient collapses to nearly zero. The
golem is answering a conditional question: once you know a state's typical
marriage age, its marriage *rate* tells you almost nothing more about
divorce.

Predictor residual plots and counterfactual plots are the two tools for
reading these golems. The first shows what a predictor adds *after* the
others are accounted for; the second asks the model to imagine intervening —
raise the marriage age, hold the rate fixed, what happens to divorce? A
regression that cannot answer a clean counterfactual is not yet a causal
model, however good its fit.

## Masking

The milk preset shows the opposite failure. Neocortex size and body mass each
look unrelated to milk energy on their own, because they are correlated with
each other and pull in opposite directions. Put both in and both light up —
the golem finally sees each effect with the other held still. Confounds hide
effects as readily as they fake them.

## Forge it

The 5H problems run both traps: spurious associations to dissolve and masked
ones to uncover. For each, draw the DAG first, decide what to condition on
*before* fitting, then check the golem against your reasoning. The
Multivariable Golem is earned by getting the graph right, not the code.
