States with more Waffle Houses have higher divorce rates. The correlation is
real, strong, and completely stupid: Waffle House is a Southern institution,
the South marries young, and marrying young predicts divorce. The waffles are
innocent bystanders standing near the crime. This chapter is about golems
that can tell a bystander from a cause — and about how easily they fail to.

The word for what the waffles are doing is **spurious association**: two
variables move together, yet neither moves the other. Some third thing —
here, being in the South — pushes on both, and the golem, which only ever
sees the numbers, cannot smell the difference between "moves together" and
"causes". Correlation is cheap. Most correlations in any dataset tell you
nothing about what would happen if you intervened, and intervening is
usually what you wanted to know about.

## More is not better

The reflex when a variable looks spurious is to "control for" everything in
sight. Throw age, marriage rate, region, and waffles into one regression and
let the golem sort it out. Sometimes this works. Sometimes it manufactures
confounds, hides real effects, or flips a sign. A regression coefficient is
not "the effect of X"; it is "the effect of X, given exactly the other
things in this particular model", and that clause does enormous, invisible
work. Add or remove one covariate and every other coefficient is answering
a different question than it was a moment ago — same names, new meanings,
no warning printed anywhere.

## Draw the causes first

Before any regression, draw what you think causes what. A directed acyclic
graph (DAG — the name is friendlier than it sounds) is nothing more
than circles for variables and arrows for direct causes, with one rule: no
loops. What makes it useful is that it is a set of claims precise enough to
be wrong. Once the arrows are down, simple rules tell you which paths
between two variables carry association and which are blocked, before you
fit anything. The sandbox below lets you condition on nodes and watch the
doors open and close. Start with waffles: which variables must you hold
fixed to ask honestly whether Waffle Houses cause divorce?

Two rules do most of the work, and they hinge on two shapes. A **fork** is
a common cause: some variable Z with arrows out to both X and Y, like the
South pushing on both waffles and divorce. Forks create spurious
association, and conditioning on the fork (holding Z fixed, comparing
only like with like) closes the path. This is legitimate control, and it
is what dissolves the waffle correlation. A **collider** is the mirror
image: a common *effect*, with arrows pointing in. Conditioning on a
collider *opens* a path that was closed, inventing association from
nothing. The difference between wisdom and self-sabotage is knowing which
kind of node you just clicked, and nothing in the data will tell you —
only the arrows do.

## Multiple regression, read honestly

Fit divorce on marriage rate alone and it looks important. Add median age at
marriage and the marriage-rate coefficient collapses to nearly zero. Neither
model malfunctioned. The golem is answering a conditional question: once
you know a state's typical marriage age, its marriage *rate* tells you
almost nothing more about divorce. Marriage rate predicted divorce only
because both are downstream of age at marriage — a fork, exactly the waffle
pattern, and adding the fork to the model is what exposed it.

Predictor residual plots and counterfactual plots are the two tools for
reading these golems. The first shows what a predictor adds *after* the
others are accounted for: strip out of marriage rate everything that age
already explains, and see whether the leftover still predicts divorce (it
doesn't). The second asks the model to imagine intervening — raise the
marriage age, hold the rate fixed, what happens to divorce? That question
is different in kind from "what do high-age states look like?", because
intervening breaks the arrows pointing *into* the variable you grabbed. A
regression that cannot answer a clean counterfactual is not yet a causal
model, however good its fit.

## Masking

The milk preset shows the opposite failure: a real effect that hides
instead of a fake one that shows. Neocortex size and body mass each look
unrelated to milk energy on their own. The reason is that the two
predictors are correlated with each other and pull the outcome in opposite
directions — bigger-brained species make richer milk, bigger-bodied species
make thinner milk, and big brains tend to come in big bodies. Examined one
at a time, each predictor's effect is cancelled by its hidden passenger.
Put both in the model and both light up, because the golem can finally see
each effect with the other held still. Confounds hide effects as readily
as they fake them, and only the model with both predictors could tell you
which was happening.

## Forge it

The 5H problems run both traps: spurious associations to dissolve and masked
ones to uncover. For each, draw the DAG first, decide what to condition on
*before* fitting, then check the golem against your reasoning. The
Multivariable Golem is earned by getting the graph right, not the code.
