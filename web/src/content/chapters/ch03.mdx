Chapter 2 left you holding a posterior — a whole curve of plausibility over
$p$. Now comes the awkward question every client, reviewer, and coauthor
will ask: *so what's the answer?* A curve is a bad sentence. This bench
teaches the honest compression: draw samples from the posterior, then ask
your questions of the samples.

The move sounds like a shortcut and is actually a philosophy. Integrals
become head-counts. Tail areas become `mean(samples < 0.2)`. Every summary
you will ever need — intervals, point estimates, predictions — is a one-liner
over a vector of draws. MCMC, waiting in Chapter 9, produces *only*
samples, so you are learning the permanent interface early.

## Draw ten thousand globes

<WebRRunner
  caption="Sampling the globe posterior (6 W in 9, flat prior)"
  code={`p_grid <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)
posterior <- dbinom(6, size = 9, prob = p_grid)
posterior <- posterior / sum(posterior)
set.seed(100)
samples <- sample(p_grid, prob = posterior, size = 1e4, replace = TRUE)

mean(samples)                      # posterior mean
mean(samples > 0.5)                # Pr(mostly water)
quantile(samples, c(0.055, 0.945)) # 89% percentile interval`}
  rows={10}
/>

Each sample is a candidate globe, drawn as often as the posterior finds it
plausible. The vector `samples` *is* your posterior now, for every
practical purpose. Interrogate it rudely.

## Intervals are summaries, never estimates

Two interval species live in the wild. A **percentile interval** chops
equal mass off both tails; it is symmetric in probability and what most
people mean by default. A **highest posterior density interval** is the
narrowest region holding the stated mass; it hugs the peak and matters when
the posterior is lopsided. For the globe they nearly coincide. For skewed
posteriors they part company, and which one you report becomes a real
choice — so report which you chose.

Two habits worth adopting from day one. First, the interval is a summary
of the posterior, never the inference itself; when the two conflict, the
posterior wins. Second, 89% is as defensible as 95% — the only virtue 95%
has is seniority, and seniority is how the flowchart happened.

## Simulating prediction

The posterior speaks about parameters. To speak about *future data*, push
whole posterior draws through the likelihood — every plausible globe gets
tossed again, in proportion to its plausibility:

<WebRRunner
  caption="Posterior predictive for nine new tosses"
  code={`p_grid <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)
posterior <- dbinom(6, size = 9, prob = p_grid)
posterior <- posterior / sum(posterior)
set.seed(100)
samples <- sample(p_grid, prob = posterior, size = 1e4, replace = TRUE)

w <- rbinom(1e4, size = 9, prob = samples)
table(w) / 1e4`}
  rows={9}
/>

Compare that spread against `rbinom(1e4, 9, 0.64)` — prediction from the
single best $p$. The predictive distribution is wider, because it carries
two uncertainties at once: the binomial's ordinary noise *and* your doubt
about $p$ itself. Golems that report only the first kind are how confident
nonsense gets published.

$$
\Pr(\tilde{W} \mid \text{data}) = \int \Pr(\tilde{W} \mid p)\,\Pr(p \mid \text{data})\,dp
$$

<Gloss>
Future tosses, averaged over every globe the posterior still believes in.
The integral looks solemn; `rbinom(1e4, 9, samples)` is the same sentence
in a working-class accent.
</Gloss>
