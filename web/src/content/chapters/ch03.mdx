Chapter 2 left you holding a posterior — a whole curve of plausibility over
$p$. Now comes the awkward question every client, reviewer, and coauthor
will ask: *so what's the answer?* A curve is a bad sentence. This bench
teaches the honest compression: draw samples from the posterior, then ask
your questions of the samples.

Sampling sounds like it should lose information, and it does lose a
little. But look what it buys. Every question you might ask of a posterior
curve is, in its native mathematical form, an integral: the probability
that $p$ is below 0.2 is the area under the curve to the left of 0.2.
Integrals are unpleasant, and they get rapidly worse as models grow. But if
you hold ten thousand samples drawn from that same curve, the integral
collapses into counting: what fraction of your samples are below 0.2? That
is `mean(samples < 0.2)`, one line, no calculus. The move sounds like a
shortcut and is actually a philosophy. Integrals become head-counts. Every
summary you will ever need — intervals, point estimates, predictions — is a
one-liner over a vector of draws. MCMC, waiting in Chapter 9, produces
*only* samples, so you are learning the permanent interface early.

Each sample is a candidate globe, drawn as often as the posterior finds it
plausible. A value of $p$ the posterior loves shows up thousands of times
in the vector; a value it doubts shows up rarely; a value it has ruled out
never appears. So the vector `samples` *is* your posterior now, for every
practical purpose — a bag of plausible worlds you can reach into whenever
you have a question. Interrogate it rudely.

## Intervals are summaries, never estimates

An interval is a way of saying "the plausible values live roughly here" in
two numbers instead of a curve. Two interval species live in the wild. A
**percentile interval** chops equal mass off both tails: for an 89%
interval, drop the lowest 5.5% of samples and the highest 5.5%, and report
what remains. It is symmetric in probability and what most people mean by
default. A **highest posterior density interval** is instead the narrowest
region holding the stated mass; it hugs the peak. For a posterior shaped
like a lopsided hill, most of its mass piled near one end, the two give
visibly different answers, and the percentile interval can even exclude the single
most plausible value. For the globe they nearly coincide. For skewed
posteriors they part company, and which one you report becomes a real
choice — so report which you chose.

Two habits worth adopting from day one. First, the interval is a summary
of the posterior, never the inference itself; when the two conflict, the
posterior wins. If someone asks a question the interval can't answer, go
back to the samples and answer it directly. Second, 89% is as defensible
as 95% — the only virtue 95% has is seniority, and seniority is how the
flowchart happened.

## Simulating prediction

The posterior speaks about parameters — about $p$, the unseen dial of the
world. But parameters are not observations. Nobody will ever hand you a
$p$; they will hand you tosses. To speak about *future data*, push whole
posterior draws through the likelihood: for each sampled globe, toss it
nine more times and record what happens. Every plausible globe gets tossed
again, in proportion to its plausibility.

Commit before the code answers. Last bench you sketched the posterior for
$p$ itself; this is a different animal — the share of water in nine *new*
tosses. A globe of known wetness can still land dry five times in a row.
Wider than the posterior, or narrower? Draw it.

Compare that spread against `rbinom(1e4, 9, 0.64)` — prediction from the
single best $p$, as if you were certain of it. The honest predictive
distribution is wider, because it carries two uncertainties at once: the
binomial's ordinary noise (even a known coin surprises you) *and* your
doubt about $p$ itself. Collapsing the second kind, by pretending your
best estimate is the truth, is one of the most common errors in applied
statistics, and it always errs in the same direction: too confident.
Golems that report only the first kind of noise are how confident nonsense
gets published.

$$
\Pr(\tilde{W} \mid \text{data}) = \int \Pr(\tilde{W} \mid p)\,\Pr(p \mid \text{data})\,dp
$$

<Gloss>
Future tosses, averaged over every globe the posterior still believes in.
The integral looks solemn; `rbinom(1e4, 9, samples)` is the same sentence
in a working-class accent.
</Gloss>

This simulation trick has a second job that matters even more than
forecasting: checking the model against the data you already have. Simulate
data from the fitted golem and set it beside the real data. Where the two
disagree — the real data has streaks the simulation never shows, or spreads
the simulation can't reach — the small world is missing something the large
world contains. No p-value required; just looking, carefully, at what your
own model claims the world is like.
