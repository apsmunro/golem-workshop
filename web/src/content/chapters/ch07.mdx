A sixth-degree polynomial can pass through twelve points almost perfectly.
It will also predict the thirteenth worse than a straight line would. Both
halves of that sentence are true at once, and the whole chapter lives in the
gap between them. Fit to the sample you have is cheap. Skill on the sample
you don't have yet is the only thing a golem is for.

Ulysses had to sail between two monsters. Ours are overfitting, the golem
that memorizes its training data down to the noise, and underfitting, the
golem too rigid to learn even the signal. Neither monster announces itself:
the overfit golem arrives waving its excellent in-sample scores.

## Scoring a golem honestly

To say "predicts well" out loud you need a currency. Information theory
supplies it. The uncertainty in a distribution of outcomes is its entropy:

$$
H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i
$$

<Gloss>
Add up, for each possible outcome, how surprising it is — weighted by how
often it happens. Rare events are big surprises; certain events are none.
</Gloss>

From entropy comes the distance between your model and the truth, the
Kullback–Leibler divergence: the extra surprise you suffer by using the
wrong distribution to predict the world. You never know the truth, so you
can never compute your distance to it — but you can compare two models'
distances, because the unknowable part cancels. What remains is the log
score: the log probability your golem assigned to what actually happened.
Deviance is that score times −2, an accounting convention from older wars.
Lower is better, and in-sample deviance lies in exactly the direction you'd
expect: more parameters, lower deviance, always.

## Estimating skill without new data

You rarely get to wait for the thirteenth point. Two families of tricks
estimate out-of-sample skill from the sample you have.

Cross-validation holds points out, fits without them, and scores the golem
on what it never saw. The game above does this exactly — leave one out,
refit, repeat. At scale, refitting is too slow, so Pareto-smoothed
importance sampling (PSIS) approximates leave-one-out from a single fit,
and complains — through its Pareto k diagnostic — about the specific points
it cannot vouch for. Those complaints are useful: a big k marks an outlier
your model finds unbelievable.

Information criteria take the other road: start from the training score and
charge a penalty for flexibility. WAIC's penalty is the summed variance of
each point's log score across the posterior — parameters only cost you to
the extent the data left them free to wiggle. In practice PSIS-LOO and WAIC
agree so often that disagreement is itself a diagnostic.

## Regularization, or teaching the golem doubt

A prior that gently disbelieves large coefficients makes a golem harder to
impress with noise. Tighten the slope priors in any model from chapter 4
and its train deviance worsens while its test deviance improves — up to a
point, past which the golem is too skeptical to learn the signal. Priors
are a dial, and the dial trades the two monsters against each other. This
is why the multilevel models of chapter 13 predict so well: they learn how
much to regularize from the data itself.

## The compass is not a captain

The sharpest lesson in the chapter: predictive skill and causal truth are
different targets. The fungus experiment from chapter 6 makes the point
brutally — the model that conditions on the post-treatment variable
*predicts better* (fungus really does tell you about growth) while being
causally useless for deciding whether to treat. LOO and WAIC rank golems by
prediction. If your question is intervention, the DAG outranks the compass.
Compare models to understand them; do not let a score pick your model for
you.
