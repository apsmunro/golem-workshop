A sixth-degree polynomial can pass through twelve points almost perfectly.
It will also predict the thirteenth worse than a straight line would. Both
halves of that sentence are true at once, and the whole chapter lives in the
gap between them. Fit to the sample you have is cheap. Skill on the sample
you don't have yet is the only thing a golem is for.

The gap exists because your twelve points are part signal, part accident —
the real relationship plus the day's noise. A flexible enough curve cannot
tell the two apart, so it learns both, faithfully. The signal will show up
again in tomorrow's data. The accidents won't; tomorrow brings fresh
accidents of its own, and every wiggle the curve devoted to yesterday's
noise is now aimed at nothing.

Ulysses had to sail between two monsters. Ours are overfitting, the golem
that memorizes its training data down to the noise, and underfitting, the
golem too rigid to learn even the signal. Neither monster announces itself:
the overfit golem arrives waving its excellent in-sample scores.

## Scoring a golem honestly

To say "predicts well" out loud you need a currency. Information theory
supplies it, and the underlying idea is only this: a good prediction is one
that is rarely surprised. The uncertainty in a distribution of outcomes is
its entropy:

$$
H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i
$$

<Gloss>
Add up, for each possible outcome, how surprising it is — weighted by how
often it happens. Rare events are big surprises; certain events are none.
</Gloss>

From entropy comes the distance between your model and the truth, the
Kullback–Leibler divergence: the extra surprise you suffer by using the
wrong distribution to predict the world. Predict rain with the wrong
probabilities and you will be surprised more often than the weather itself
requires; that excess surprise is the divergence, and a perfect model has
none. You never know the truth, so you can never compute your own distance
to it — but you can compare two models' distances, because the unknowable
part (the weather's own inherent surprise) is the same for both and
cancels. What remains is the log score: the log probability your golem
assigned to what actually happened, event by event. A golem that assigned
high probability to the things that occurred was, in the only sense that
matters, less wrong. Deviance is that score times −2, an accounting
convention from older wars. Lower is better, and in-sample deviance lies in
exactly the direction you'd expect: more parameters, lower deviance,
always — which is precisely why in-sample deviance cannot be the judge.

## Estimating skill without new data

You rarely get to wait for the thirteenth point. Two families of tricks
estimate out-of-sample skill from the sample you have.

Cross-validation holds points out, fits without them, and scores the golem
on what it never saw — a rehearsal of the real test, staged with data you
already own. The purest version leaves out one point at a time: fit on
eleven, predict the twelfth, rotate, and total the scores. The game above
does this exactly. At scale, refitting once per data point is too slow, so
Pareto-smoothed importance sampling (PSIS) approximates leave-one-out from
a single fit — and, better, complains when the approximation itself gets
shaky. Its Pareto k diagnostic names the specific points it cannot vouch
for, and those complaints are useful in their own right: a big k marks an
outlier so influential that removing it visibly moves the model, which is
something you want to know regardless.

Information criteria take the other road: start from the training score,
which you know flatters the model, and charge a penalty for flexibility to
cancel the flattery. WAIC's penalty is the summed variance of each point's
log score across the posterior — parameters only cost you to the extent
the data left them free to wiggle, so a tightly constrained parameter is
nearly free while a loose one bills at full rate. In practice PSIS-LOO and
WAIC agree so often that disagreement is itself a diagnostic: when they
split, something (usually an extreme observation) deserves your
attention.

## Regularization, or teaching the golem doubt

A prior that gently disbelieves large coefficients makes a golem harder to
impress with noise. The mechanism is nothing new — it is the same prior
arithmetic from Chapter 2. A skeptical prior demands more evidence before
it lets a coefficient grow, and noise, which by definition doesn't repeat,
rarely musters that evidence, while real signal does. Tighten the slope
priors in any model from chapter 4 and its train deviance worsens while
its test deviance improves — up to a point, past which the golem is too
skeptical to learn the signal. Priors are a dial, and the dial trades the
two monsters against each other. This is why the multilevel models of
chapter 13 predict so well: they learn how much to regularize from the
data itself.

## The compass is not a captain

The sharpest lesson in the chapter: predictive skill and causal truth are
different targets. The fungus experiment from chapter 6 makes the point
brutally — the model that conditions on the post-treatment variable
*predicts better* (fungus really does tell you about growth) while being
causally useless for deciding whether to treat. There is no contradiction:
knowing the fungus level genuinely improves your forecast of the harvest,
and simultaneously ruins your estimate of whether treating caused the
harvest. LOO and WAIC rank golems by prediction. If your question is
intervention, the DAG outranks the compass. Compare models to understand
them; do not let a score pick your model for you.
