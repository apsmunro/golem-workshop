Ptolemy's model of the heavens was wrong about almost everything and
predicted eclipses beautifully. Circles riding on circles — epicycles — can
trace any closed orbit to arbitrary precision, so the geocentric golem fit
the sky it was shown while placing the Earth in the middle of nothing. Linear
regression is our epicycle: a device that approximates almost any mean
relationship, often useful, rarely a claim about mechanism. Keep the Ptolemy
warning close as you build this one, because it fits so willingly.

## Why heights are a bell

Add up many small independent nudges — genes, meals, illnesses, a thousand
of them — and the sum forgets where each nudge came from. All that survives
is a mean and a spread. That is the Gaussian, and it is not a law of nature
so much as a law of ignorance: the least committal shape for a quantity
built from many additions. The golem uses it not because heights *are*
normal but because, knowing only a mean and a variance, normal is the most
honest guess.

$$
h_i \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma)
$$

<Gloss>
Each height is a draw from one bell, with center μ and width σ. No predictor
yet — just the claim that heights scatter around a typical value.
</Gloss>

## Priors are claims; simulate them

Before fitting anything, hear what a prior says out loud. A slope prior of
Normal(0, 10) sounds humble and is in fact unhinged: it cheerfully proposes
people who shrink to negative height or tower past three metres. Move the
sliders until the golem stops predicting monsters.

<Wide><PriorPlayground /></Wide>

A LogNormal(0, 1) slope forces the relationship positive and keeps it
plausible — taller-with-heavier, at a believable rate. This is the whole
argument for weakly informative priors, and you just watched it rather than
took it on faith.

## The linear golem

Give the mean a predictor. Center weight on its own mean so the intercept
stays interpretable — it becomes "the height of an average-weight person",
not "the height of a weightless one".

$$
h_i \sim \text{Normal}(\mu_i, \sigma), \qquad \mu_i = \alpha + \beta\,(x_i - \bar{x})
$$

## Reading the posterior

Fit it and the golem hands back not a line but a posterior over lines. Here
is that posterior for 352 !Kung adults — the real brms draws, fit offline
with Stan and shipped as thinned samples (and if those ever fail to load, an
in-browser quadratic approximation stands in). Marginals
first, then the model's lines over the data, then a counterfactual: pick a
weight the census never recorded and ask for a height.

<Wide><HowellExplorer /></Wide>

Watch the two bands. The narrow brass one is uncertainty about the *mean*
line — it tightens with more data toward a hairline. The wide plum one is
uncertainty about an actual *person*, and it never collapses, because people
scatter around the mean no matter how sure you are of the mean. Confusing
these two bands is how forecasts end up sounding certain about individuals
when they are only certain about averages.

## Predict before you look

The 4H problems push this golem where it breaks: extrapolate past the data
and it predicts confidently wrong, because a line is a local approximation
wearing a global disguise — Ptolemy again. Sketch what you expect the
weight-height relationship to look like for children before you fit it; the
curvature will surprise you, and being surprised on purpose is the point.
