Ptolemy's model of the heavens was wrong about almost everything and
predicted eclipses beautifully. Circles riding on circles — epicycles — can
trace any closed orbit to arbitrary precision, so the geocentric golem fit
the sky it was shown while placing the Earth in the middle of nothing. Linear
regression is our epicycle: a device that approximates almost any mean
relationship, often useful, rarely a claim about mechanism. Keep the Ptolemy
warning close as you build this one, because it fits so willingly.

What regression actually does is easy to state. You have an outcome you
care about (height, say) and a predictor you suspect matters (weight).
The golem's job is to describe how the *average* height changes as weight
changes, and how much individuals scatter around that average. Two jobs,
two kinds of number, and confusing them causes trouble all the way to the
end of this chapter.

## Why heights are a bell

Add up many small independent nudges — genes, meals, illnesses, a thousand
of them — and the sum forgets where each nudge came from. All that survives
is a mean and a spread. That is the Gaussian, the bell curve, and it is not
a law of nature so much as a law of ignorance: the least committal shape
for a quantity built from many additions. The golem uses it not because
heights *are* normal but because, knowing only a mean and a variance,
normal is the most honest guess.

You can watch this happen with coins. Flip sixteen coins, count heads,
repeat a thousand times, and histogram the counts: a bell, already. Nothing
about a single coin is bell-shaped; the bell comes from the adding. Any
outcome assembled the same way — many small influences, each pushing a
little up or a little down — will tend toward the same shape, which is why
the Gaussian keeps showing up in places that owe each other nothing.

$$
h_i \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma)
$$

<Gloss>
Each height is a draw from one bell, with center μ and width σ. No predictor
yet — just the claim that heights scatter around a typical value.
</Gloss>

Read the notation slowly once, because every model from here on is written
in it. The left side names the data. The tilde says "is distributed as" —
a claim about scatter, not an equation to solve. The right side names the
distribution and its parameters, the unknown dials the golem will learn:
here μ, the center, and σ, the spread. A model is a stack of lines like
this, one per assumption, and you should be able to read each line aloud
as an English sentence about the world.

## Priors are claims; simulate them

Every parameter needs a prior — a statement of what values were plausible
before the data arrived. Newcomers often treat priors as an embarrassment
to be minimized. The better posture: a prior is a claim about the world,
and like any claim it can be inspected. The inspection method is
simulation. Draw parameter values from the prior, push them through the
model, and look at the fake people it invents. If the prior invents
impossible people, it was a bad claim, and you found out for free — before
any data, with no risk of cheating.

Hear what a prior says out loud. A slope prior of Normal(0, 10) sounds
humble — centered on zero, letting the data decide! — and is in fact
unhinged: it cheerfully proposes people who shrink to negative height or
tower past three metres. Move the sliders until the golem stops predicting
monsters.

A LogNormal(0, 1) slope forces the relationship positive and keeps it
plausible — taller-with-heavier, at a believable rate. Notice that this is
knowledge you already had: you have met people, and none of them lost
height by gaining weight. A prior that encodes what everyone knows is not
bias; refusing to encode it means your model starts out believing in
monsters. This is the whole argument for weakly informative priors, and
you just watched it rather than took it on faith.

## The linear golem

Now give the mean a predictor. The move is small but it is *the* move of
this entire book: μ stops being a single number and becomes a function of
weight, so the golem now claims that people of different weights have
different average heights, arranged along a line. One practical trick
makes the result readable: center weight — subtract its own mean — so the
intercept stays interpretable. It becomes "the height of an average-weight
person", not "the height of a weightless one".

$$
h_i \sim \text{Normal}(\mu_i, \sigma), \qquad \mu_i = \alpha + \beta\,(x_i - \bar{x})
$$

The parameters have plain readings. α is the expected height at average
weight. β is the slope: how many centimetres of average height come with
one extra kilogram. And σ still measures how far real individuals scatter
around the line — the line never claims people sit on it, only that they
cluster near it.

## Reading the posterior

Fit it and the golem hands back not a line but a posterior over lines —
thousands of (α, β, σ) triples, each one a complete candidate story about
heights, each held with plausibility proportional to how well it explains
the data. Here is that posterior for 352 !Kung adults — the real brms
draws, fit offline with Stan and shipped as thinned samples (and if those
ever fail to load, an in-browser quadratic approximation stands in).

Watch the two bands. The narrow brass one is uncertainty about the *mean*
line — where the average sits. It tightens with more data toward a
hairline, because averages are easy to pin down with enough people. The
wide plum one is uncertainty about an actual *person*, and it never
collapses, no matter how much data arrives, because people genuinely
scatter around the mean and no amount of certainty about the average
removes the scatter. A weather service can be nearly sure the average July
is warm and still be humble about next Tuesday. Confusing these two bands
is how forecasts end up sounding certain about individuals when they are
only certain about averages.

## Predict before you look

The 4H problems push this golem where it breaks: extrapolate past the data
and it predicts confidently wrong, because a line is a local approximation
wearing a global disguise — Ptolemy again. Sketch what you expect the
weight-height relationship to look like for children before you fit it; the
curvature will surprise you, and being surprised on purpose is the point.
