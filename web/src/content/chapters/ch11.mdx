Count data are where the comfortable habits of Gaussian regression go to
die. The outcome is 0, 1, 2, 3 — never 2.4, never −1 — and the natural
scale of effects is multiplicative, not additive. This chapter forges the
two count golems that carry most of applied statistics: the binomial for
counts with a known ceiling (seven successes out of ten tries), the
Poisson for counts without one (how many tools a society invents). Both
are maximum-entropy choices from chapter 10; both speak through links; and
both punish anyone who reads their coefficients as if they were slopes on
the outcome.

That last warning deserves one concrete airing before the animals arrive.
In chapter 4, a slope of 0.9 meant "one more kilogram, 0.9 more
centimetres", everywhere, for everyone. A logistic coefficient of 0.9
means "one more unit of x adds 0.9 to the log-odds" — and how much that
moves the *probability* depends entirely on where the probability already
was. Near 50%, a lot; near 99%, almost nothing. There is no single "effect
on the outcome" to quote. You must pick a starting point, push the
predictor, and report what happens — which is why this chapter keeps
shoving you toward the outcome scale and the contrast.

## Chimpanzees and the lever

Seven chimpanzees, two levers, and a question: does a partner across the
table make the prosocial lever more attractive? Each pull is a Bernoulli
trial — a binomial with a ceiling of one, a single yes-or-no — the
treatment is one of four side-and-partner combinations, and every animal
gets its own intercept because chimpanzees, like people, have habits.
Actor 2, as you are about to see, has nothing but habits.

<Rosetta
  model="m11.4"
  book={`m11.4 <- ulam(
  alist(
    pulled_left ~ dbinom(1, p),
    logit(p) <- a[actor] + b[treatment],
    a[actor] ~ dnorm(0, 1.5),
    b[treatment] ~ dnorm(0, 0.5)
  ), data = dat_list, chains = 4, log_lik = TRUE)`}
  brms={`m11.4 <- brm(
  pulled_left ~ 0 + factor(actor) + factor(treatment),
  data = d, family = bernoulli(),
  prior = c(
    prior(normal(0, 1.5), class = b),
    prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = b, coef = "factortreatment2"),
    prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = b, coef = "factortreatment3"),
    prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = b, coef = "factortreatment4")
  ),
  chains = 4, cores = 4, backend = "cmdstanr", seed = 1959
)`}
/>

Why Normal(0, 1.5) and not something flatter? You saw the reason in the
link morpher: on the logit scale, wide is not humble. Normal(0, 10) on an
intercept says the chimp either always pulls left or never does — a prior
piled against the walls of probability.

The contrasts hug zero on both scales: adding a partner moved no lever. A
contrast, note, is the difference between two conditions computed draw by
draw from the posterior: the direct answer to "how much did the
treatment change behavior?", which no single coefficient supplies.
Meanwhile actor 2 pulled left all 72 times, and the data alone would push
its intercept to infinity — no finite log-odds makes 72-for-72 anything
but more likely still. The prior is what keeps the estimate merely
enormous rather than infinite, sane rather than silly. The scale toggle is
the chapter's repeated warning label: a log-odds effect and a probability
effect are different numbers, and only the second one answers "how much
did behavior change?"

## Berkeley, and which question you asked

The 1973 Berkeley admissions data show men admitted at a higher rate than
women. They also show, department by department, no such advantage — in
the largest departments, a small one for women. Neither summary is a
mistake. The two analyses answer two different questions, and the fork in
the road is a variable in the middle: which department a person applied
to.

<Wide><AdmitParadox /></Wide>

The gender-only model estimates the *total* effect of gender, and the
total effect is real: it flows through which departments people apply to,
because the departments women favored admitted almost nobody of anyone.
Condition on department and you ask the *direct* question instead — does
gender matter for two applications sitting in the same pile? Different
estimand, different answer, opposite sign. Both numbers are correct;
they measure different paths through the same graph, and which one you
want depends on what you would intervene on. The paradox dissolves the
moment you say out loud which causal question you are paying the golem to
answer. Chapter 6's back-door lessons did not retire when the outcomes
became integers.

## Tools, people, and the ledger's fine print

For counts with no ceiling, the binomial hands over to the Poisson. It is
the binomial pushed to a limit (many opportunities, each individually
unlikely) and it travels light: one parameter λ, serving as both mean and
variance, linked through the log so that predictors act multiplicatively.
A coefficient of 0.3 on the log scale means each unit of the predictor
scales the expected count by e^0.3 ≈ 1.35 — a 35% increase, compounding,
never an addition.

<Rosetta
  model="m11.10"
  book={`m11.10 <- ulam(
  alist(
    T ~ dpois(lambda),
    log(lambda) <- a[cid] + b[cid] * P,
    a[cid] ~ dnorm(3, 0.5),
    b[cid] ~ dnorm(0, 0.2)
  ), data = dat2, chains = 4, log_lik = TRUE)`}
  brms={`m11.10 <- brm(
  total_tools ~ 0 + contact + contact:log_pop_std,
  data = d, family = poisson(),
  prior = c(
    prior(normal(3, 0.5), class = b, coef = contacthigh),
    prior(normal(3, 0.5), class = b, coef = contactlow),
    prior(normal(0, 0.2), class = b, coef = "contacthigh:log_pop_std"),
    prior(normal(0, 0.2), class = b, coef = "contactlow:log_pop_std")
  ),
  chains = 4, cores = 4, backend = "cmdstanr", seed = 1959
)`}
/>

<Wide><KlinePoisson /></Wide>

Ten societies is not much data, so the priors do visible work — again
tuned by simulating on the outcome scale, since Normal(0, 10) on a log
link proposes societies with more tools than atoms. Hawaii sits alone at
the population extreme, low contact, 71 tools, daring the model to
extrapolate. And the monastery ledger beneath teaches the offset, the
Poisson's bookkeeping clause. When observations cover different
exposures (one monastery logs manuscripts daily, another weekly) the raw
counts are not comparable: seven versus five may be the same underlying
rate wearing different calendars. The fix is to put the exposure into the
model as log τ added to the linear predictor, which converts every count
back to a rate before comparison. Skip the offset and you are comparing
λ·7 to λ·1 and calling it a difference in diligence.

## Forge it

The 11E problems drill the scale gymnastics: log-odds to odds to
probability and back, until the logit stops feeling like a cipher. 11M
covers why aggregated and disaggregated binomials fit the same posterior
but different information criteria, and what an offset is doing under the
hood. The 11H set turns you loose on the real datasets — chimpanzees
re-analyzed, Kline with priors loosened, the Berkeley data interrogated
properly. Finish them and the Integer Golem takes its bench.
