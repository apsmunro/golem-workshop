Every posterior so far came from a polite fiction: that the whole
distribution is one multivariate Gaussian hill, measurable by its peak and
curvature. Chapters 4 through 8 got away with it because their posteriors
really are that shape. The models ahead — hundreds of parameters, funnels,
ridges — are not, and no formula will hand you their posteriors. What
remains is to *visit* the posterior instead of solving it: take samples
whose long-run frequencies match the distribution, then work with samples
exactly as you have since chapter 3.

Pause on how strange and how practical that is. You cannot write down the
posterior. You cannot even evaluate how tall it is at a given point without
knowing a normalizing constant that is itself the impossible integral. But
you *can* wander through parameter space by rules that guarantee one thing:
in the long run, the time you spend in each region is proportional to that
region's posterior probability. Keep a diary of the walk and the diary is
your sample — the same kind of object `sample()` gave you in chapter 3,
earned the hard way. That family of wandering rules is Markov chain Monte
Carlo, MCMC.

The book tells this as the parable of a king condemned to visit islands in
proportion to their populations, with a coin-flip rule that needs no map of
the whole archipelago. That rule — propose, compare, sometimes move — is
the Metropolis algorithm, and it works. Slowly. Each proposal is blind, and
in high dimensions blind proposals land in probabilistic nowhere almost
every time. Why? Because in a space of a hundred dimensions, nearly all the
volume is far from where the probability lives, so a random step in a
random direction almost always steps off a cliff and gets rejected. The
random walk dawdles; consecutive samples barely differ; a million draws can
carry a few hundred draws' worth of information.

## Roll, don't stumble

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo stops guessing. Turn the posterior upside down so
probability becomes depth: now it is a bowl, and the current sample is a
marble resting in it. Flick the marble with random momentum and let physics
run. The marble rolls fast through the deep, banks up the walls, slows in
the shallows — and wherever it stops is the next proposal, likely far from
where it started yet still in credible territory, because gravity did the
navigation. The cost is that you must know the slope under the marble at
every instant: HMC needs gradients, which is why it lives inside Stan and
not inside a coin flip.

<Wide><HmcToy /></Wide>

The simulation is discrete: the marble advances in L leapfrog steps of size
ε. Set ε small with generous L and trajectories hug the bowl beautifully at
real computational cost. Push ε up and the discretization stops conserving
energy — the marble gains speed from nowhere and flies out of the bowl.
That is a divergence. It is not a warning label; it is a physical event you
just watched, and when Stan reports one it means exactly this: somewhere in
your posterior there is terrain the simulated marble could not cross
honestly, and the samples near it may not be trustworthy. Try the funnel:
divergences pile up in the neck, where curvature changes too fast for any
single ε. Chapter 13 meets that geometry in earnest and learns to melt it
away by reparameterization.

A chain is one marble's diary — one independent walk through the
posterior, started from its own random spot. Warm-up is not burn-in with
better branding: during those first thousand iterations Stan is *tuning
the sampler itself*, adapting ε and the mass matrix to the posterior's
shape, and those draws are discarded because they were taken with the
wrong instrument. Run several chains from dispersed starts. The logic is
plain: if four walkers who began in different corners of parameter space
all end up describing the same territory, the territory is probably real.
Agreement among strangers is the only convergence evidence there is.

## Chain care

Two numbers summarize the health of a run. R̂ compares variance between
chains to variance within them; at convergence the chains are
indistinguishable (a stranger reading the diaries could not tell which
walker wrote which) and R̂ approaches 1. This workshop's pipeline refuses
anything above 1.01. Effective sample size counts draws after discounting
autocorrelation. Consecutive MCMC samples are correlated — each is a step
from the last, not a fresh draw — so a thousand sticky draws may carry the
information of fifty independent ones, and it is the fifty that determines
how precise your estimates are. But numbers are summaries of pictures, and
you should be able to read the pictures. Diagnose these patients:

A healthy trace is a hairy caterpillar: stationary, well-mixed, three
chains braided into one band. The ailments each have a silhouette — a
flatline, ribbons that never meet, clay ticks crowding one region, an
approach path left in the sample. When a chain misbehaves, the fix is
rarely "more iterations". It is better geometry: tighter priors,
reparameterization, a model that says what you actually believe. A golem
that samples badly is usually a golem that was asked a badly posed
question — a flat prior on an unbounded parameter, two parameters doing
one job, a likelihood that cannot tell its inputs apart. The sampler is
the first tool in the workshop that complains out loud. Listen to it.

## Forge it

The 9E problems check that you can say *why* — why HMC beats a random walk,
why discrete parameters are off the menu, why R̂ near 1 is necessary but
never sufficient. 9M has you refit m9.1 under different priors and warm-up
budgets and watch the diagnostics move. The 9H problems include the
devil's funnel and the two-legs model that HMC diagnoses honestly where
quap would have smiled and lied. From this chapter on, every golem in the
workshop runs on this engine.
